What are the SEBI Rules for Specialised Investment Funds (SIFs) in India?

Specialised Investment Fund Financial Insights
By Rajat Monga Updated Apr 10, 2026
Add ET Money as a preferred source on Google

Share this story

Key Takeaways

  • Specialised Investment Funds (SIFs) are introduced under the SEBI (Mutual Funds) Regulations, 1996.
  • Minimum investment is ₹10 lakh, calculated at the PAN level across all SIF strategies of an AMC.
  • The minimum threshold does not apply to accredited investors.
  • SIFs can take up to 25% unhedged derivative exposure, within overall exposure limits.
  • Total gross exposure of a SIF strategy cannot exceed 100% of its net assets.
  • Close-ended and interval SIF strategies must be listed on stock exchanges.
  • SIFs must disclose portfolio details every alternate month and evaluate their Risk-Band monthly.
  • A standard SEBI-mandated risk disclaimer must appear in all SIF advertisements.

What are Specialised Investment Funds (SIFs)?

SEBI-regulated Specialised Investment Funds are a new category of investment products introduced under the SEBI (Mutual Funds) Regulations, 1996.

They are created to bridge the gap between Mutual Funds and Portfolio Management Services (PMS).

  • They operate under a separate regulatory framework approved by SEBI.
  • They can use certain advanced investment strategies, including limited short exposure through derivatives.
  • They must have a distinct identity and branding separate from regular mutual fund schemes.

Who Can Invest in Specialised Investment Funds?

Only investors who meet SEBI’s minimum investment requirement can invest in Specialised Investment Funds (SIFs).

  • The investor must invest at least ₹10 lakh across all SIF investment strategies of an AMC. Hence, it makes SIFs more suitable for HNIs.
  • The ₹10 lakh requirement is calculated at the PAN level, meaning all SIF investments under the same PAN are combined.
  • This minimum investment rule does not apply to accredited investors.

What Is the Minimum Investment Requirement for SIFs?

The minimum investment required in Specialised Investment Funds (SIFs) is ₹10 lakh per investor.

  • The ₹10 lakh threshold applies across all SIF investment strategies of an AMC.
  • It applies only to SIF investments.
  • Investments in regular mutual fund schemes are not included in this calculation.

Systematic options like SIP, SWP and STP are allowed, but the total investment must meet the ₹10 lakh minimum requirement.

How Does SEBI Monitor Minimum Investment Compliance in SIFs?

Asset Management Companies (AMCs) must monitor the ₹10 lakh minimum investment requirement on a daily basis.

Active breach

If an investor’s investment falls below ₹10 lakh because they redeem part of their units, it is not allowed. The AMC must ensure this does not happen.

Passive breach

If the investment value falls below ₹10 lakh due to a fall in NAV, it is allowed. However, in such cases, the investor can only redeem the entire remaining amount.

AMFI, stock exchanges, depositories and RTAs must put systems in place to actively monitor compliance.

What Investment Restrictions Apply to SIFs?

SEBI has set clear limits on how much a Specialised Investment Fund (SIF) can invest in a single issuer or a single sector.

Issuer-wise limits (Debt and Money Market Securities)

  • Up to 20% of NAV in securities rated AAA
  • Up to 16% of NAV in securities rated AA
  • Up to 12% of NAV in securities rated A and below

These limits can be increased by up to 5% of NAV, but only with prior approval from the trustees and the AMC board.

Sector-level limit

A SIF cannot invest more than 25% of its NAV in debt and money market securities of a single sector.

Are SIFs Allowed to Invest in Derivatives?

Yes, Specialised Investment Funds (SIFs) are allowed to invest in derivatives, subject to limits set by SEBI.

  • A SIF can take up to 25% of its net assets as unhedged exposure through exchange-traded derivatives.
  • This 25% limit is separate from derivative exposure taken for hedging or portfolio rebalancing.
  • Unhedged short exposure means taking a short position through derivatives without holding the underlying asset.

How Is Derivative Exposure Calculated in SIFs?

SEBI has prescribed clear formulas to calculate derivative exposure in Specialised Investment Funds (SIFs). 

Exposure is calculated based on the contract value.

  • Futures (long or short): Futures price × Lot size × Number of contracts
  • Options bought: Option premium paid × Lot size × Number of contracts
  • Options sold: Market price of the underlying × Lot size × Number of contracts

For any other type of derivative, exposure is calculated as the notional market value of the contract.

What Is the Maximum Total Exposure Allowed in SIFs?

The total cumulative gross exposure of a Specialised Investment Fund (SIF) cannot exceed 100% of its net assets at any point in time.

This total exposure includes investments in:

  • Equity
  • Debt
  • Derivatives (including commodity and fixed income derivatives)
  • REITs and InvITs
  • Repo transactions
  • Credit default swaps and other permitted securities

How Do Subscription and Redemption Work in SIFs?

Specialised Investment Funds (SIFs) can be structured as open-ended, close-ended, or interval investment strategies.

  • Open-ended strategies allow ongoing subscription and redemption.
  • Close-ended strategies allow subscription during the offer period and redemption at maturity.
  • Interval strategies allow subscription and redemption at specified intervals.

SEBI allows different subscription and redemption frequencies.

For example, a SIF strategy may allow daily subscriptions but weekly redemptions.

Are Notice Periods Allowed for Redemption in SIFs?

Yes, SEBI allows Asset Management Companies (AMCs) to impose notice periods for redemption in Specialised Investment Funds (SIFs), based on the liquidity of the strategy.

  • The investor will receive redemption proceeds based on the NAV at the end of the notice period.
  • The notice period cannot exceed 15 working days.

Are SIF Units Listed on Stock Exchanges?

Yes, units of certain Specialised Investment Fund (SIF) strategies must be listed on recognised stock exchanges.

  • Listing is mandatory for close-ended investment strategies.
  • Listing is also mandatory for interval investment strategies.

Listing provides an exit option to investors who wish to sell their units on the exchange.

How Are SIF Investment Strategies Benchmarked?

Specialised Investment Fund (SIF) strategies must follow a single-tier benchmark structure. The different types of SIFs are benchmarked accordingly.

  • Equity-oriented strategies must be compared with a suitable broad market index such as BSE Sensex, NSE Nifty, BSE 100 or CRISIL 500.
  • Debt-oriented strategies must be compared with a suitable broad market index that represents the portfolio.
  • Hybrid strategies must be compared with a suitable broad market benchmark, wherever available.

An AMC may also provide a second-tier benchmark at its discretion.

Who Can Distribute Specialised Investment Funds?

Entities that are already engaged in the sale and distribution of mutual fund products can distribute Specialised Investment Funds (SIFs), subject to additional certification.

  • The distributor must pass the NISM Series-XIII: Common Derivatives Certification Examination.
  • AMFI and the AMC must ensure that their agents and distributors meet this requirement.

What Disclosures Are Mandatory for SIFs?

SEBI requires Specialised Investment Funds (SIFs) to disclose important information in their offer documents so that investors can make informed decisions.

The offer documents must clearly disclose:

  • Subscription and redemption frequency
  • Notice period, if applicable
  • Frequency of portfolio disclosure
  • Scenario analysis for derivative positions
  • Details of derivative exposure, including maximum limits for exposure other than hedging and portfolio rebalancing
  • Liquidity risk management tools and their applicability
  • Any other relevant information

What Is the Risk-Band in SIFs?

The Risk-Band is a pictorial risk meter that shows the level of risk associated with a Specialised Investment Fund (SIF) strategy.

SEBI has prescribed five risk levels:

  • Risk Band Level 1 – Lowest Risk
  • Risk Band Level 2
  • Risk Band Level 3
  • Risk Band Level 4
  • Risk Band Level 5 – Highest Risk

The Risk-Band helps investors understand the potential risk of the investment strategy.

How Often Is the Risk-Band Reviewed and Disclosed?

The Risk-Band of a Specialised Investment Fund (SIF) strategy must be evaluated every month.

  • It must be disclosed within 10 days from the end of each month on the AMC’s website and AMFI’s website.
  • As on March 31 each year, SIFs must disclose the risk level along with the number of times the risk level changed during the year.
  • Any change in the Risk-Band must be communicated to investors through a notice and by email or SMS.

How Frequently Is the SIF Portfolio Disclosed?

Specialised Investment Funds (SIFs) must disclose their portfolio every alternate month.

  •  Portfolio must be disclosed as on the end of May, July, September, November, January and March.
  • The disclosure must include ISIN details and derivative instruments.
  • The portfolio must be published within 10 days from the end of the month on the AMC website and the AMFI website.

What Disclaimer Is Mandatory for SIF Advertisements?

SEBI has prescribed a standard disclaimer that must be included in all advertisements and promotional materials of Specialised Investment Funds (SIFs).

The disclaimer must read exactly as follows:

“Investments in Specialized Investment Fund involves relatively higher risk including potential loss of capital, liquidity risk and market volatility. Please read all investment strategy related documents carefully before making the investment decision.”

No words can be added, removed or modified in this disclaimer.

Are SIFs Suitable for All Investors?

Specialised Investment Funds (SIFs) are not designed for all investors. They involve relatively higher risk and require a minimum investment of ₹10 lakh.

  • They permit limited unhedged derivative exposure.
  • They involve market volatility and liquidity risk.
  • They require a higher investment commitment compared to regular mutual fund schemes.

Investors should carefully weigh the risks and benefits of SIFs before investing.
